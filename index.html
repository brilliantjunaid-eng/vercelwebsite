The phone isn't the enemy. Depleted dopamine is. When you scroll for thirty minutes before opening your textbook, you've already spent the neurochemical currency your brain needs to find studying rewarding.

Every other productivity app assumes you can already begin. They give you timers, streaks, and checklists — all things that help once you're working. They have nothing to say to the student staring at the ceiling.

Threshold works at the moment before work. It uses curiosity — a scientifically documented precursor to the same dopamine circuits as reward — to create the urge to study before the session even begins.

The curiosity hook

You tell us what you're
studying. We show you
why it's extraordinary.

Before your session begins, Threshold shows you one carefully crafted sentence about your topic. Not a summary. Not a fun fact. The most surprising destination of that subject — glimpsed from the doorway before you walk in.

Mathematics — Complex Numbers
The equation eiπ + 1 = 0 connects five of the most fundamental constants in all of mathematics in a single line — and you're about to learn why that's even possible.

The hook doesn't explain itself. It creates anticipatory dopamine — the brain enters a state of wanting to know more before the work even begins. The curiosity is the entry point.

The session arc

Every session follows
a deliberate shape.

01
The Pact
Set the baseline
Before anything else, you make a self-promise. What will you avoid this session — no music, no phone, no layering of high-stimulation activities before studying. This is a ritual. It primes the brain consciously before work begins.
02
Curiosity Hook
Receive the hook
Enter your topic. Threshold responds with a single sentence that makes the subject surprising. Not an explanation — an invitation. Your brain begins to want to know, before effort is even required.
03
Task Queue
Break the session into tasks
A simple, minimal task queue. Add what you plan to do in this session. No complexity, no project hierarchy — just enough structure to give the session a shape.
04
Dopamine Thermostat
Adaptive check-ins
Every 25 minutes, a single question: how are you feeling right now, 1–10? One tap. Three seconds. Based on your answer, Threshold adapts — more stimulation when you're low, silence when you're already in flow. It never adds fuel to a fire that's already burning.
05
Retrieval Challenge
One hard question at the end
At the close of your session, Threshold asks a genuinely difficult question about what you just studied. Not recall — genuine retrieval. The difficulty is the point: Robert Bjork's research shows that the harder the retrieval effort, the larger the dopamine response when you succeed. Finishing should feel earned.
06
Session Reflection
Close the curiosity loop
One final question: "What surprised you today?" This is not journalling for its own sake. It closes the curiosity loop your brain opened at the start of the session, reinforces memory encoding, and ends the session on discovery rather than exhaustion.
The dopamine thermostat

Stimulation calibrated
to your actual state —
not a fixed schedule.

Most apps layer rewards on top of whatever state you're in. Threshold treats your neurochemical state like a thermostat — it only acts when the temperature moves out of range.

1–4
Low state
You're struggling to engage. Threshold offers a small stimulation — a new curiosity hook on the same topic, a surprising connection to something you already know, or a quiet reframe of what you're doing and why. Nothing loud. Just enough to shift the gear.
5–6
Moderate state
You're present but not quite locked in. A mild nudge — a reframe of the task ahead, or a small question that re-engages your curiosity about the material without pulling you out of the session.
7–10
Flow state
You're in it. Threshold steps back. A calm acknowledgement — "you're in a good state, keep going" — and then silence. Adding stimulation to an already high state leads to crashes and burnout. The app knows when to disappear.
The graduation arc

The only app designed
to make itself unnecessary.

Every other productivity tool wants your daily retention. Threshold has a different success metric: the moment you no longer need it.

Week 1–2
Entry ramp
The app meets you where you are — with a depleted dopamine baseline from doomscrolling — and competes with your phone using mild external rewards. More hooks, more check-ins, more visible encouragement. It earns your attention before it asks for your effort.
Week 2–4
Quiet pullback
Based on your check-in data, the app begins reducing stimulation when it detects your state during studying is consistently high without having just received a reward. That signal means intrinsic motivation is beginning to grow. The pullback follows your data, not a fixed schedule.
Month 2+
Quiet companion
Less stimulation, more trust. The structure remains but the scaffolding is lighter. You begin generating your own curiosity naturally — the app is no longer providing it, only creating space for it.
Eventually
Graduation
The app suggests you try a session without it. "You've had 7 strong sessions this week. Try one tomorrow without opening us first. See how it feels." Graduation is the goal. Students who reach this stage are the success metric — not retention numbers.
The design principle
"We are explicitly designing toward our own obsolescence. Every feature asks: does this build the student's capacity, or replace it?"

This is the core philosophical difference from every competitor. Habit-forming apps are built to be irreplaceable. Threshold is built to be outgrown. It believes the struggle and effort of learning is itself the reward — and its entire design is oriented around helping the student feel that truth, not just be told it.

The neuroscience

The intelligence is
under the hood.

Gruber & Ranganath, 2014
Curiosity activates the same dopamine circuits as external reward
The neuroscience behind the curiosity hook. Genuine curiosity — not gamification — produces the same anticipatory dopamine as receiving a prize. Threshold uses this to create motivation before effort, not after.
Huberman Lab
Attaching reward to effort rather than outcome
The Pact, the session arc, and the thermostat are all built around this principle. The app trains the brain to find the act of studying rewarding — not the grade at the end — by shaping how each session begins and closes.
Robert Bjork — Desirable Difficulties
Harder retrieval produces larger rewards
The end-of-session retrieval question is deliberately difficult. Bjork's research shows that the effort required to retrieve knowledge amplifies the dopamine response when retrieval succeeds. Finishing should feel genuinely earned.
What Threshold is not

Every category this
isn't — and why.

Khan Academy / Learning platforms
Threshold does not teach content. It teaches the brain to want to learn — and then gets out of the way of whatever learning tool the student uses.
Notion / Note-taking tools
Threshold does not store information passively. The architecture is about session state, not knowledge management.
Habitica / RPG gamification
No fantasy mechanics, no skins, no XP systems. The intelligence is invisible. The student should feel the app understands them without being able to articulate why.
Forest / Guilt-based focus apps
Guilt and visual metaphor are not dopamine. Watching a tree die is not the same as genuinely wanting to study. Threshold competes for attention at the neurochemical level, not the aesthetic one.
