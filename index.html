Dopamine Thermostat
Adaptive check-ins during the sprint
Every 25 minutes, a single question: how are you feeling right now, 1–10? One tap. Three seconds. Based on your answer, Pact adapts — more stimulation when you're low, silence when you're already in flow. It never adds fuel to a fire that's already burning.

At the close of your session, Pact asks a genuinely difficult question about what you just studied. Not recall — genuine retrieval. The difficulty is the point: Robert Bjork's research shows that the harder the retrieval effort, the larger the dopamine response when you succeed.

One final question: "What surprised you today?" This closes the curiosity loop your brain opened at the start, reinforces memory encoding, and ends the session on discovery rather than exhaustion.
The dopamine thermostat

Stimulation calibrated
to your actual state —
not a fixed schedule.

Most apps layer rewards on top of whatever state you're in. Pact treats your neurochemical state like a thermostat — it only acts when the temperature moves out of range.

1–4
Low state
You're struggling to engage. Pact offers a small stimulation — a new curiosity hook, a surprising connection, or a quiet reframe of what you're doing and why. Nothing loud. Just enough to shift the gear.
5–6
Moderate state
Present but not quite locked in. A mild nudge — a reframe of the task ahead, or a small question that re-engages curiosity without pulling you out of the session.
7–10
Flow state
You're in it. Pact steps back. A calm acknowledgement — "you're in a good state, keep going" — and then silence. Adding stimulation to an already high state leads to crashes. The app knows when to disappear.
The graduation arc

The only app designed
to make itself unnecessary.

Every other productivity tool wants your daily retention. Pact has a different success metric: the moment you no longer need it.

Week 1–2
Entry ramp
The app meets you where you are — with a depleted dopamine baseline from doomscrolling — and competes with your phone using mild external rewards. More hooks, more check-ins, more visible encouragement. It earns your attention before it asks for your effort.
Week 2–4
Quiet pullback
Based on your check-in data, Pact begins reducing stimulation when it detects your state during studying is consistently high without having just received a reward. That signal means intrinsic motivation is beginning to grow. The pullback follows your data, not a fixed schedule.
Month 2+
Quiet companion
Less stimulation, more trust. The structure remains but the scaffolding is lighter. You begin generating your own curiosity naturally — the app is no longer providing it, only creating space for it.
Eventually
Graduation
Pact suggests you try a session without it. "You've had 7 strong sessions this week. Try one tomorrow without opening us first. See how it feels." Students who reach this stage are the success metric — not retention numbers.
The design principle
"We are explicitly designing toward our own obsolescence. Every feature asks: does this build the student's capacity, or replace it?"

This is the core philosophical difference from every competitor. Habit-forming apps are built to be irreplaceable. Pact is built to be outgrown. It believes the struggle and effort of learning is itself the reward — and its entire design is oriented around helping the student feel that truth, not just be told it.

The neuroscience

The intelligence is
under the hood.

Gruber & Ranganath, 2014
Curiosity activates the same dopamine circuits as external reward
Genuine curiosity — not gamification — produces the same anticipatory dopamine as receiving a prize. Pact uses this to create motivation before effort, not after. The curiosity warm-up isn't decoration — it's neurochemistry.
Huberman Lab
Attaching reward to effort rather than outcome
The Pact, the session arc, and the thermostat are all built on this principle. The app trains the brain to find the act of studying rewarding — not the grade at the end — by shaping how each session begins and closes.
Robert Bjork — Desirable Difficulties
Harder retrieval produces larger rewards
The end-of-session retrieval question is deliberately difficult. Bjork's research shows that the effort required to retrieve knowledge amplifies the dopamine response when retrieval succeeds. Finishing should feel genuinely earned.
